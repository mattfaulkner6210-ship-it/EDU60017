From surface to deep: six ways to get there

Instead of asking students to recall a fact, ask them to explain it: why does this work, how does it connect to what we already know? Elaborative questioning forces learners to build reasoning instead of just retrieving a label.

In reciprocal teaching, students take turns leading small-group discussion through four roles: summarising, questioning, clarifying and predicting. It's one of the higher-impact strategies in the research because it forces active processing rather than passive listening.

Get students to physically map how ideas relate, rather than just listing them. Deep learning is fundamentally about linking new knowledge to prior understanding and spotting patterns — concept mapping makes those links visible instead of assumed.

Effective feedback isn't “well done” — it's specific and improvement-focused, like pointing a student toward linking their evidence back to their thesis rather than just confirming an answer is correct. Feedback aimed at reasoning nudges students past surface accuracy.

Revisiting a concept in different contexts over time — rather than one intensive session — consolidates surface knowledge into something transferable. Spaced, varied practice is one of the most reliable ways to help learning stick beyond the test.

Prompting students to evaluate their own understanding — what do I actually know, what am I still unsure about — builds the self-awareness that sits at the heart of deep learning. Metacognitive reflection is often the strategy that ties all the others together.
